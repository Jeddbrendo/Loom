Ancient Hebrew Calendar

The Ancient Hebrew calendar is a lunisolar system historically tied to agricultural seasons rather than purely astronomical calculations.

Year 1 Epoch

Based on biblical chronology, Year 1 is calibrated to the creation of Adam in 4026 BCE. (Note: This differs from the modern Rabbinic Jewish calendar which sets Year 1 at 3761 BCE).

Spring New Year

Unlike the Gregorian calendar which starts in winter, the sacred calendar year begins in the spring with the month of Nisan (Abib), overlapping mid-March to mid-April.

Lunisolar Drift & Veadar

Twelve lunar months (29 or 30 days) fall 11 days short of a solar year. To prevent the months from drifting entirely out of the seasons, a 13th leap month—Veadar—was periodically added. Because ancient leap months were often declared by physical observation (e.g., checking if barley was ripe), they cannot be mapped with perfect historical mathematical precision.

Timeline Application

To provide a smooth visual experience across thousands of years without erratic grid jumping, this timeline normalizes the lunar months into equal solar blocks tied to the Spring Equinox. Visually, "Nisan" will always predictably overlap late March on the Gregorian scale.
